7th Sustainable Nanotechnology Organization Conference 2018 (past event)

Thursday, November 8 - Saturday, November 10, Alexandria, VA

Hilton Alexandria Old Town, Alexandria, Virginia 22314

The 2018 SNO conference sessions represented a hybrid of topics on selected "systems" that contribute to sustainability and more traditional topics. Sessions included talks addressing applications, effects and implications, analytical methods, and lifecycle aspects of nanomaterials within each system. The aims were to identity where nanomaterials and nanotechnology can improve the sustainability of each system and to foster integration of knowledge between applications and implications within each system. We also aimed to encourage papers on the applications of nanomaterials to sustainability. Papers on lifecycle aspects of nanomaterials within each system or across systems were encouraged. New developments in analytical methods and instrumentation were also received. Topics from prior conferences were addressed under the current session topics.

As always, in all our sessions, we ask how we are helping reach sustainability through nanotechnology:

2018 CONFERENCE SESSIONS

  1. Tribute to Barbara Karn
    Barbara Karn is co-founder and executive director of SNO. She retired from NSF where she directed a program funding research in environmental nanotechnology. Prior to that, she spent 10 years building the environmental nanotechnology community—applications and implications- through grants at EPA. She spent a year working on green nanotechnology at the Wilson Center. Dr. Karn represented EPA on the NNI and brought nano environmental and human health issues to the European Community. She is most proud of being able to maintain this research community through the legacy of SNO.
  2. Food/Agriculture
    This session centered on Nanotechnology for food production, pesticide delivery, nutrient delivery, disease suppression, food fortification, and environmental implications.
  3. Water
    This session focused on applications of nanotechnology to address water or air contaminants, including applications in water supply, wastewater treatment and industrial air pollution control.
  4. Fate and Exposure
    This session considered studies addressing nanomaterial release fate and transport, transformations, and exposure modelling.
  5. Ecotoxicology
    This session emphasized the evaluation of the effects (i.e. positive or negative) that advanced nanomaterials have on the environment and human health.
  6. Life Cycle
    It is important to consider a life-cycle perspective when evaluating the applications of nanotechnology, from raw materials to synthesis, and from product use to end of life. Papers in this session addressed the entire life-cycle of particular applications, or the implications in specific life-cycle phases, including recycling.
  7. Sensors/Measurement
    Papers in this session focus on the need to develop sensors, new instruments, approaches, and/or further refinement of existing tools for characterizing nanomaterials and using nanomaterials as sensors to detect chemicals of interest.
  8. Green Synthesis
    This session focuses on the synthesis of nanomaterials with lowered energy and fewer polluting by-products and starting materials. Environmentally benign methods for forming in place, incorporating lithography, vacuum coating and spray coating; mechanical synthesis (‘top-down’ method that reduces the size of particles by attrition, for example, ball milling or planetary grinding); gas phase synthesis, such as plasma vaporization, chemical vapor synthesis and laser ablation; and green wet chemistry. Green chemistry techniques are used here such as use of Non-toxic Solvents (e.g., supercritical CO2, water), Self-assembly, Solid state processes, Bio-inspired nanoscale synthesis, Templating processes, Molten Salt or Ionic Liquid Synthesis, Microwave technology, Improved synthesis, fewer steps, Photochemical synthesis, Use of renewables, and many more material and energy saving methods.
  9. Education/Social aspects
    Papers in this session will address nano-education programs and curriculum development. In addition, societal aspects, such as laws, regulations, economics, and social issues, will be covered.
  10. Nanomedicine/Alternative Test Methods
    This session will accept papers that deal with the use of various types of nanoparticles for use in medicine, particularly to diagnose and treat cancer. Research papers on alternatives to animal testing are encouraged.     >
